# 1 Introduction

Price fraud is not a new phenomenon, but the scale at which it operates in modern e-commerce platforms has outpaced the institutional machinery designed to contain it. Discount fabrication — raising a product's listed price before a promotional event and then advertising the inflated figure as the original — has become so widespread that Chinese regulators documented hundreds of enforcement cases within a single year. Discriminatory pricing, in which different consumers are shown different prices for the same product based on inferred purchasing power or platform loyalty, quietly erodes consumer trust without leaving obvious traces. Misleading discount stacks, where multiple promotional labels are combined in ways that make the effective price nearly impossible to verify at a glance, exploit cognitive limitations rather than any outright misrepresentation. These violations share a structural feature: they are difficult to detect in real time, easy to dispute after the fact, and costly to investigate manually at the scale of millions of daily transactions.

This thesis investigates whether a combination of large language models (LLMs), retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), and multi-step agent workflows can meaningfully improve the automation of price compliance reasoning — not as a replacement for regulatory judgment, but as a tool that reduces the latency and inconsistency of preliminary screening. Three technical routes are systematically compared on a unified benchmark built from 791 real administrative penalty documents, producing a set of empirical findings about where each approach adds value, and where all three fall short.

## 1.1 Research Background and Significance

### 1.1.1 The Problem Space: Price Violations in E-Commerce

Chinese price law draws a relatively clear boundary around what constitutes a pricing violation. The *Price Law of the People's Republic of China* (1997) and subsequent regulations — most notably the *Measures for the Administration of Price Violations* and the more recent guidance from the State Administration for Market Regulation (SAMR) — prohibit practices such as operating without clearly marked prices (不明码标价), charging amounts beyond a displayed price (标价外加价), violating government-set price ceilings (政府定价违规), and using misleading price indicators that create false impressions about value or discount depth (误导性价格标示).

In practice, these categories blur. A product page on a major e-commerce platform may simultaneously exhibit unclear price marking because the base price is buried beneath a promotional banner, and misleading price display because the "original price" shown on that banner was never actually charged at scale. Inspectors reviewing such a page must hold multiple regulatory standards in mind at once, cross-reference the applicable provisions, and produce a written determination with a clear legal basis. The cognitive load is non-trivial, and the volume of listings makes systematic manual review impossible. Platform self-regulation exists but operates under obvious incentive conflicts.

The consequences of under-enforcement are concrete. Consumers who make purchase decisions based on fabricated discounts lose money directly. Those subject to discriminatory pricing lose it invisibly. At the aggregate level, unfair pricing practices distort market competition and erode confidence in online retail — an outcome that is difficult to reverse once normalized. From the regulator's perspective, the current model is predominantly reactive: a complaint is filed, an inspector is assigned, evidence is gathered, and a determination is made, often weeks or months after the event. By that point, the pricing behavior may have been modified, the promotional window is closed, and the consumer's recourse is effectively limited to token compensation.

The latency problem is compounded by inconsistency. Different regional enforcement offices apply the same statutory provisions with different degrees of rigor. Determinations about whether a specific pricing pattern constitutes "misleading price display" depend heavily on the inspector's familiarity with the case law and their reading of ambiguous statutory language. A system capable of rapidly generating a preliminary legal analysis — grounded in the actual text of relevant provisions and anchored by precedent from past enforcement actions — could at minimum create a consistent first-pass screening layer that human reviewers then refine.

### 1.1.2 The Regulatory Context and Its Digitalization Gap

The scale of China's e-commerce market makes this problem structurally different from the equivalent challenge in traditional retail. As of the mid-2020s, major platforms host hundreds of millions of active product listings, with pricing updated continuously by algorithms rather than by individual decisions. The regulatory apparatus, however, remains staffed by human inspectors whose throughput is bounded by working hours and cognitive bandwidth. A persistent gap has opened between the velocity of pricing behavior and the speed at which that behavior can be evaluated.

Several initiatives have addressed adjacent problems. Price monitoring dashboards aggregate price history data to flag anomalous swings. Consumer complaint platforms provide a channel for self-reporting. Some platforms have introduced internal compliance tooling, though the specifics are rarely disclosed publicly. None of these approaches, however, addresses the core challenge: given a description of a specific pricing behavior, which provisions of Chinese price law apply, does the behavior constitute a violation, and if so, what remediation should be required?

Answering that question is fundamentally a legal reasoning task. It requires understanding natural language descriptions of pricing scenarios, retrieving the applicable statutory text, applying general principles to specific facts, and producing a structured output that can be used in an enforcement workflow. This is precisely the type of task for which LLMs have shown promising capabilities — and precisely the type of task for which their known failure modes (hallucination, unsupported legal citations, reasoning that sounds plausible but is factually wrong) impose the greatest risk. The question of how to harness these models' strengths while managing their failure modes in a compliance-critical setting is what motivates the architectural exploration undertaken in this thesis.

### 1.1.3 The Promise and Limits of Large Language Models

The past several years have seen rapid advances in the legal reasoning capabilities of general-purpose LLMs. Models in the GPT-4 class and their open-source counterparts perform reasonably on bar examination problems, statutory interpretation exercises, and compliance classification tasks when evaluated in English. Performance on Chinese legal text has historically lagged but has improved substantially with models trained on larger Chinese-language corpora.

The fundamental limitation of pure LLM inference in compliance applications is not, in the first instance, reasoning quality — it is grounding. A model generating a legal analysis from memory may produce a citation to a provision that does not exist, or cite a real provision whose text does not actually support the conclusion drawn. In price compliance specifically, the distinction between "the model thinks this provision applies" and "this provision's actual text, retrieved from an authoritative source, supports this determination" is legally significant. Enforcement actions must cite specific articles; a determination grounded in a misremembered or fabricated provision is worse than no determination at all, because it creates a record that can be challenged.

RAG addresses the grounding problem by retrieving relevant documents at inference time and conditioning the model's output on their content. Agents extend this further by enabling multi-step reasoning, self-checking, and structured workflows that decompose a complex compliance determination into manageable sub-tasks. Whether these architectural additions produce meaningful practical gains — in this domain, on this task — is an empirical question that cannot be settled by theoretical argument alone. This thesis provides a direct answer through controlled comparison on a real-world benchmark derived from actual administrative enforcement records, a methodological choice that distinguishes it from evaluations conducted on synthetic or hypothetical scenarios.

## 1.2 Research Status at Home and Abroad

### 1.2.1 LLMs for Legal and Compliance Applications

The application of language models to legal tasks has a longer history than the current generation of LLMs might suggest. Early work used feature-based classifiers and recurrent neural networks for judgment prediction and case outcome modeling. The arrival of transformer-based pre-trained models shifted the paradigm toward fine-tuning general models on domain-specific corpora, and the arrival of instruction-tuned LLMs made zero-shot and few-shot legal reasoning feasible for a broader range of tasks.

In the Chinese legal domain, two systems have received particular attention. ChatLaw [1] adapts large pre-trained models to Chinese legal text through supervised fine-tuning on a curated corpus of statutes, regulations, and case records, demonstrating improvements on legal question-answering benchmarks relative to general-purpose baselines. LawGPT pursues a similar approach with somewhat different training data construction and evaluation methodology. Both systems represent genuine progress in building models sensitive to the vocabulary and reasoning patterns of Chinese law.

Their limitation, from the perspective of this work, lies in generality. Systems designed and evaluated on broad legal domains — covering criminal, civil, and administrative law across many sub-areas — are not optimized for the specific provisions, enforcement patterns, and evidentiary standards of price regulation. A model that has learned that "misleading" is a legally loaded term in consumer protection law may still fail to correctly identify which specific article of the *Pricing Violations Measures* applies to a particular e-commerce scenario, and may be unaware of how regional enforcement offices have interpreted ambiguous provisions in past administrative penalty decisions. Domain specificity matters, and the price compliance domain remains largely unaddressed in the published literature.

### 1.2.2 Retrieval-Augmented Generation

The formal introduction of RAG as an architecture can be dated to the work of Lewis et al. [2], who demonstrated that combining a dense retrieval component with a sequence-to-sequence generator substantially improved performance on knowledge-intensive NLP tasks compared to generation from model parameters alone. The core insight — that factual grounding benefits from access to an external, updatable knowledge store rather than relying on knowledge compressed into model weights — proved durable and has since been extended in multiple directions.

Vanilla RAG, as the Lewis et al. formulation is now called, retrieves a fixed number of documents based on query-document similarity and appends them to the generation prompt. This approach has predictable weaknesses: retrieval quality determines generation quality, a noisy retrieval set degrades output even when the model itself is capable, and the fixed retrieval window cannot adapt to queries that require more or less context than the default. Subsequent work addressed these problems along several axes.

Self-RAG [3] introduced the notion of adaptive retrieval, where the model itself generates reflection tokens to decide when retrieval is needed and whether retrieved passages are relevant and supportive of the generation. This substantially reduces unnecessary retrieval overhead and improves the model's ability to decline retrieval when its parametric knowledge is sufficient. Corrective RAG (CRAG) [4] added an explicit document quality assessment step and a fallback to web search when the initially retrieved documents fail a relevance threshold — a design that addresses the degraded-retrieval scenario directly. GraphRAG [5] departed further from the flat document retrieval paradigm by constructing knowledge graphs from the corpus and enabling retrieval at the level of entities and relations, which is particularly advantageous for domains where the relationships between concepts matter as much as the concepts themselves.

Alongside these architectural advances, hybrid retrieval methods — combining sparse keyword-based retrieval (BM25 and its variants) with dense vector retrieval, fused via reciprocal rank fusion or learned combination weights — have become standard practice for production-quality systems. Re-ranking with cross-encoder models, which evaluate query-document relevance more precisely than the bi-encoder models used for initial retrieval, adds a second stage that further improves the quality of the context passed to the generator. The system developed in this thesis draws on this trajectory, implementing hybrid retrieval with RRF fusion and CrossEncoder re-ranking, along with a dynamic filtering step that adapts the number of retrieved documents to the characteristics of the retrieval result set.

### 1.2.3 LLM-Based Agents

The use of LLMs as reasoning engines within multi-step agent workflows emerged as a distinct research direction around 2022, building on two foundational ideas. Chain-of-Thought prompting [6] demonstrated that eliciting explicit intermediate reasoning steps — rather than expecting the model to jump directly from problem statement to answer — substantially improved performance on complex reasoning tasks, including tasks requiring multi-step logical inference. This finding held across model scales, though larger models benefited more.

The ReAct framework [7] extended this idea by interleaving reasoning steps with actions — tool calls, database lookups, web searches — allowing the model to ground its reasoning in information gathered during execution rather than relying entirely on what it could recall or derive from the initial prompt. This paradigm proved particularly effective for tasks where the correct action at any step depends on information not available at the outset, which describes compliance reasoning reasonably well: the relevant provisions may not be obvious until the violation type is characterized, and the appropriate case precedents depend on which provisions apply.

Subsequent work on planner-executor-critic architectures added reflexive components: a module that evaluates the output of a reasoning step and either accepts it or triggers re-reasoning. LangChain and LangGraph [8] provided engineering frameworks that made these multi-step workflows practical to implement and evaluate at scale, abstracting away much of the orchestration complexity. The six-node agent architecture developed in this thesis follows this general tradition, with a linear workflow that moves from intent classification through retrieval, grading, structured reasoning, reflection, and remediation advice generation.

A recurring challenge across agent architectures is the tension between reliability and flexibility. More complex workflows with more decision points and more tool calls produce more informative outputs but are also more prone to cascading errors, and higher per-sample latency makes them harder to deploy in high-throughput settings. In our system, the Reflector node addresses the reliability concern by applying lightweight heuristic validation before accepting a reasoning output, with at most one re-reasoning retry — a deliberate choice that limits error amplification at the cost of some potential for recovery from difficult edge cases. This design reflects a broader principle that governs the agent architecture: predictability is more valuable than theoretical optimality when the system's output feeds into a regulatory workflow.

### 1.2.4 Regulatory Technology and Chinese Price Supervision Digitalization

The broader field of regulatory technology (RegTech) encompasses a range of computational approaches to compliance monitoring, risk assessment, and enforcement support. In financial services, rule-based transaction monitoring, anomaly detection, and NLP-based disclosure analysis have reached meaningful deployment. In environmental regulation, satellite imagery analysis and sensor data integration support inspection planning. Price regulation has received comparatively little attention from the academic literature, despite its practical significance.

Within China, the digitalization of market supervision has progressed along administrative rather than AI-native lines. Platforms are required to maintain price records; inspection agencies have access to price history databases; the administrative penalty documentation system (accessible at cfws.samr.gov.cn) provides a structured record of past enforcement actions. These infrastructure investments create the raw material for data-driven compliance tools, but the translation from data availability to automated reasoning is not straightforward.

To our knowledge, no published system directly addresses the task of automated price compliance reasoning from natural language scenario descriptions using the Chinese statutory and regulatory corpus as a knowledge base. The closest adjacent work focuses on consumer complaint classification (a simpler task that requires identifying a topic rather than producing a legal determination) and general Chinese legal question answering (which treats price law as one sub-domain among many rather than as the primary focus). The gap this thesis addresses is therefore not merely incremental — it reflects the absence of an established technical baseline for the specific combination of task, domain, and regulatory context. Section 1.3 describes how the benchmark and comparison methodology developed here are designed to provide that baseline.

## 1.3 Research Content and Contributions

The work reported in this thesis proceeds from a practical premise: that the value of any AI-assisted compliance tool must be assessed against a concrete task with concrete evaluation criteria, not against abstract capability claims. Accordingly, the research begins with dataset construction and ends with a systematic three-way comparison across a unified benchmark.

**Dataset.** The evaluation dataset was constructed from 791 administrative penalty documents collected from the SAMR case filing website (cfws.samr.gov.cn) using EasySpider. After parsing, deduplication, and quality filtering, 780 samples were retained: 489 labeled as violations and 291 labeled as compliant. The violation samples span four categories that correspond directly to the major provisions of Chinese price law: unpriced display (221 samples), government-priced violations (117 samples), surcharge above marked price (73 samples), and misleading price display (49 samples). The remaining cases fall into smaller violation sub-types. Constructing this dataset from real enforcement documents rather than synthetic scenarios ensures that the evaluation captures the actual language, ambiguity, and edge cases that a deployed system would encounter.

The law knowledge base contains 691 extracted articles from the relevant statutes and regulations. Separately, 133 historical enforcement cases were processed and indexed. A deliberate design choice was made to set the case retrieval parameter to zero (cases\_k=0) during formal evaluation — meaning that case-base results were excluded from the evaluation context — to prevent same-source contamination between the training-adjacent case documents and the evaluation set derived from the same corpus. This anti-contamination design is discussed in detail in Chapter 3.

**Technical routes.** Three routes are compared on the same 780-sample evaluation set. The Baseline route uses a pure LLM inference approach: the model (Qwen-8B, selected through a pilot comparison against Qwen and MiniMax) receives the scenario description and a system prompt and generates a compliance determination directly from its parametric knowledge. The RAG route augments this with the hybrid retrieval system described above, conditioning generation on retrieved law articles. The Agent route replaces the single-step generation with the six-node workflow: IntentAnalyzer (rule-based, no LLM call) → AdaptiveRetriever → Grader → ReasoningEngine → Reflector → RemediationAdvisor.

**Evaluation framework.** The metric system spans three tiers. Classification metrics — binary accuracy, violation-type accuracy, precision, recall, and F1 — capture the core task performance. Quality metrics — legal-basis quality, reasoning quality, evidence-chain completeness, legal-citation accuracy, remediation actionability, and interpretability — assess the usefulness of the generated output beyond the binary label. Cost metrics — response time and token consumption — establish the practical tradeoffs.

**Key findings.** The results reveal a picture that is more nuanced than a simple ranking of approaches. Binary accuracy varies within about 2.9 pp across the three routes: 89.35% (Baseline), 89.85% (RAG), and 86.98% (Agent). The Qwen-8B model, even without retrieval, already achieves strong classification performance on this domain. The Agent trades a small accuracy regression (~2.4 pp versus Baseline) for the highest reasoning-quality score and the only route producing actionable remediation. The reasoning-quality score rises across the three routes — 0.8415 (Baseline), 0.8685 (RAG), 0.8931 (Agent) — reflecting the progressive benefit of structured multi-step reasoning. These gains come at a cost: Agent response time averages 37.62 seconds, compared to 7.02 seconds for the Baseline, which is in practice the main tradeoff a deployment decision must weigh.

There are honest limitations to acknowledge. The heuristic scoring used in the Grader and Reflector nodes assesses structural properties of outputs rather than legal-fact correctness; a well-structured but legally wrong analysis would score better than it deserves. Producing accurate, specific statutory citations remains difficult across all three routes, even with retrieval support. Compliant cases are the common weak point, with all three routes below 82% accuracy on the negative class — the models systematically over-flag, a tendency that would require additional calibration before deployment.

In aggregate, the contributions of this thesis are: a domain-specific evaluation dataset and benchmark for Chinese price compliance reasoning; a systematic empirical comparison of three technical routes on that benchmark; a hybrid retrieval architecture with anti-contamination design validated in this setting; a six-node agent workflow with self-reflection and remediation advice generation; and a three-tier evaluation metric system designed to capture dimensions of compliance output quality beyond classification accuracy.

## 1.4 Thesis Organization

The remainder of this thesis is organized as follows. Chapter 2 introduces the theoretical foundations — the relevant provisions of Chinese price law, the technical background of LLMs and the transformer architecture, and the core concepts of RAG and agent-based reasoning — that underpin the subsequent system design. Chapter 3 describes the construction of the evaluation dataset and law knowledge base, including the document collection pipeline, parsing and annotation procedures, and the anti-contamination design for the case base. Chapter 4 presents the Baseline route in detail, covering the model selection pilot study, the prompt engineering strategy, and the baseline evaluation results. Chapter 5 develops the RAG route, with a full account of the hybrid retrieval architecture, the RRF fusion and CrossEncoder re-ranking pipeline, and the dynamic filtering mechanism. Chapter 6 describes the Agent route, walking through each of the six nodes in the workflow and the design rationale for the reflection and remediation components. Chapter 7 defines the three-tier evaluation metric system, explaining the construction and interpretation of each metric. Chapter 8 presents the system prototype and its interface, including implementation details and a brief demonstration of the deployed tool. Chapter 9 concludes with a synthesis of findings, an honest discussion of limitations, and directions for future work.
